When discussing the variables of hand-brewed coffee, we tend to focus most of our efforts on selecting top-quality specialty coffee beans, purchasing expensive flat-blade grinders, or practicing precise water injection techniques. However, one of the most critical but often overlooked facts is: **A cup of coffee contains about 98.5% to 99% "water"**.

Even if you have the best Panamanian Geisha coffee beans in the world and perfect brewing techniques, if you use poor water quality, the flavor in the final cup will still appear mediocre, dull, and even full of flaws. Water is not only a passive carrier, it is also an active chemical solvent. The mineral composition in the water directly determines which aromatic substances in the coffee powder can be successfully extracted.

This article will take you deep into the water chemistry of specialty coffee, explaining how TDS, hardness (calcium and magnesium ions), and pH buffering capacity can reshape the flavor of your coffee.

## 1. Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)

TDS is the most common water quality measurement, usually in ppm (parts per million) or mg/L. It represents the sum of all dissolved inorganic salts and trace organic matter in water except pure water (H₂O).

### Effect of TDS on Extraction Capacity
In the specialty coffee world, we think of water as an empty backpack.
- **Pure water (TDS approaches 0, such as RO reverse osmosis water or distilled water)**: This is a completely empty backpack, and its "dissolving power" is extremely strong. If you use pure water to brew coffee, the water will absorb the substances in the coffee powder like a sponge, which can easily lead to **over-extraction**. When you drink it, there is often a sharp sourness and bitterness, because the water forcibly pulls out many bad tail substances.
- **High TDS water (such as mineral-rich mineral water, TDS > 200)**: This backpack is already filled with rocks. Because the ions in the water are saturated, it lacks enough space to dissolve the aromatic substances of the coffee, which results in **under-extraction**. The brewed coffee is often bland, lackluster, dull, and heavily earthy or powdery.

**SCA (Specialty Coffee Association) Gold Standard**: Recommended target TDS for brewing water falls at **150 ppm** (acceptable range is 75-250 ppm). But please note that TDS is only the total amount. What is more important is "what minerals are contained in" these 150 ppm.

## 2. General Hardness (GH): Extracted catalyst

Total hardness mainly refers to the concentration of **calcium ions (Ca²⁺)** and **magnesium ions (Mg²⁺)** in water. These two alkaline earth metal ions are positively charged and play an extremely critical role as "flavor carriers" in the coffee extraction process.

### Magnesium Ions (Mg²⁺): The power behind fruit bombs
Coffee contains many organic compounds (such as citric acid, malic acid, etc.) that bring rich floral and fruity aromas and bright acidity. These substances usually contain oxygen atoms, and magnesium ions have a strong binding force on oxygen atoms.
If the water contains an appropriate amount of magnesium ions, it will act like a magnet, helping the hot water to more efficiently "pull out" the fruity compounds and high-quality acids in the coffee powder. Water rich in magnesium ions can usually brew coffee with distinct layers and bright acidity.

### Calcium ions (Ca²⁺): the cornerstone of sweetness and thickness
Although the extraction ability of calcium ions is not as powerful as magnesium ions, it is more inclined to extract larger molecules in coffee, bringing a thicker body and sweetness. However, if the calcium ions in the water are too high, it will not only easily form serious scale in the heating equipment, but also make the coffee taste heavy and even produce a dry, chalky taste.

**Ideal state**: A moderate calcium to magnesium ratio ensures that coffee has both bright acidity and solid sweetness.

## 3. Carbonate Hardness (KH) and buffering capacity

This may be the most difficult factor in water chemistry to understand, but it is also the factor that has the greatest impact on the "acid experience."
Carbonate hardness, or what we often call "Alkalinity", mainly refers to the concentration of **bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻)** in water.

### Why does it affect the acidity of coffee?
Coffee itself is a slightly acidic beverage (pH around 5.0). The bicarbonate ion in water is an "alkaline buffer", and its chemical property is to neutralize acid.

- **When the KH is too high (buffering capacity is too strong)**: The bicarbonate in the water will ruthlessly neutralize all the delicate fruit acids in the coffee. This will make a light-roasted Ethiopian coffee that is supposed to be full of the sour and sweet flavors of raspberries and peach taste flat, rigid, and even have a mediocre taste similar to soap.
- **When the KH is too low (almost no buffering capacity)**: There is no substance to restrain the acidity of the coffee, and the original acidity of the coffee will be released without reservation. If the beans themselves are extremely acidic, the brewed coffee may be very sour and pungent, making it difficult to drink (like drinking lemon juice).

**Recommended values ​​for SCA**: The target value for carbonate hardness is approximately **40 ppm** (acceptable range 40-75 ppm). This provides a little bit of buffering power to cut through overly harsh acidity, but leaves enough room for the alluring bright acidity of specialty coffee to shine through.

## How to get perfect brewing water?

If you find that the high-end coffee beans you buy always don't taste like the coffee shop, we strongly recommend that you check the water you use. Here are a few common water quality solutions:

1. **Filtered Tap Water (BWT / Brita)**:
   If the water quality in the area you live in is soft (such as Taipei, Taiwan), you can usually get good brewing water by using a high-quality activated carbon plus ion exchange resin filter element to filter out chlorine and impurities. If you live in an area with extremely hard water, the calcium removal effect of the water filter jug ​​may be limited.
2. **Purchase bottled pure water and prepare water quality (Third Wave Water)**:
   This is currently the most popular and precise practice in the specialty coffee world. Buy a bucket of RO purified or purified water with 0 TDS, and add a commercially available coffee-specific water formula powder (such as Third Wave Water or Lotus Water Drops). These formulas have helped you prepare the perfect ratio of calcium, magnesium, and bicarbonate.
3. **DIY Water Recipes**:
   More advanced players will go to the chemical store to buy "Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate)" and "baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)" and add them to pure water to prepare their own coffee water. This recipe, called "Barista Hustle Water Recipe", is widely circulated on the Internet, is extremely low-cost and has amazing effects.
