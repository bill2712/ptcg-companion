Among all coffee brewing equipment, if you can only choose to upgrade one item, 99% of professional baristas in the world will give you the same answer: "Buy a better grinder."

Many beginners will spend tens of thousands of yuan to buy a top-notch Italian coffee machine or a temperature-controlled hand brewing kettle, but only get an entry-level grinder worth a few thousand yuan. They often encounter a bottleneck: no matter how they adjust the water temperature, water-to-powder ratio or water injection method, the coffee is always mixed with a sharp sourness and an unpalatable bitterness.
The culprit of all this usually points to the same physical phenomenon: **Poor Grind Size Distribution**.

## What is Particle Size Distribution (PSD)?

When the coffee beans are crushed by the grinder's cutter, the coffee powder that comes out will never be a perfect sphere of exactly the same size. If you put these powders under a microscope and measure them through a laser particle size analyzer, you will get a bell-shaped curve (usually a bimodal distribution), which is the particle size distribution.

In any grind, coffee grounds are roughly divided into three volumes:
1. **Target Size**: This is the main particle size we want (for example, 600-800 microns commonly used in hand brewing).
2. **Finees**: Powders with extremely small diameters (usually less than 100 microns) that look like flour or dust to the naked eye.
3. **Boulders**: Huge particles that are much larger than the target size. Sometimes you can even see half of the coffee beans that are not completely broken.

A "good" grinder will have a grinding curve that is very steep and concentrated - that is, a very high ratio of target powder diameter and a very small ratio of fine to coarse powder (high uniformity). On the other hand, the curve of an entry-level grinder will be very flat and wide.

## Why is uniformity so important?

Coffee extraction is a chemical dissolution process based on "surface area and water contact time."

Just imagine, what would happen if you dropped a rock sugar and a small spoonful of powdered sugar into hot water at the same time? The powdered sugar will dissolve and disappear instantly, but the rock sugar may still be under the water after five minutes.

The same laws of physics apply to ground coffee:
- **Extremely fast extraction of fines**: Due to the huge surface area, fines will release all substances within the first few seconds of contact with hot water. Then, the water will begin to extract the wood fibers and tannins deep in the fine powder, resulting in extremely severe **over-extraction (bitterness, dry throat)**.
- **Extremely slow extraction of Boulders**: It is difficult for water to penetrate the center of these huge particles, so only the material on the surface is dissolved. The rich sweetness and aroma inside cannot be released, which leads to **insufficient extraction (sour, watery taste)**.

When you brew with a grinder with poor uniformity, both "over-extraction of fine powders" and "under-extraction of coarse powders" occur in your filter cup. That's why your coffee tastes sour and bitter, a confusing mix of flavors.

## The deadly killer of fine powder: channeling effect (Channeling)

In addition to causing over-extraction, fine powders also have a more terrifying physical side effect: **clogging and channeling**.

In a hand-brewed coffee filter, the water flows downward through gravity. When the coffee powder contains a large amount of fine powder, these dust-like fine powder will settle down with the water flow and get stuck in the tiny holes of the filter paper, or fill the gaps between larger particles.

This has two consequences:
### 1. Clogging
The water cannot go down at all. The water that was originally expected to drain out in 2 minutes and 30 seconds was still accumulated in the filter cup at 4 minutes. This results in a significantly longer steeping time for the entire cup of coffee, inevitably leading to overall over-extraction.

### 2. Channeling
Water is smart and will always seek the path of least resistance. When fine powder blocks the bottom of the filter cup, water pressure will find a fragile breakthrough inside the powder layer. Once the water finds a gap, a large amount of water will concentrate and rush down from that channel (Channel).
This means:
- **Coffee powder near the channel**: It is washed away by a large amount of hot water, severely over-extracted, and bitter water is released.
- **Coffee powder outside the channel**: There is almost no contact with the flowing hot water, showing insufficient extraction.

This is the worst case scenario for hand-brewed coffee, and it’s a physical limitation that no matter how much you improve your pouring skills, you can’t save it.

## Cutterhead Geometry: Flat Knife vs. Taper Knife

Bean grinders on the market are mainly divided into two types of cutterhead structures, which produce completely different particle size distribution characteristics:

- **Flat Burrs**: Two parallel discs that use centrifugal force to push the coffee beans outward and cut them. Flat knives usually produce a very concentrated "unimodal distribution" with extremely high uniformity and very little fines. This allows the flat knife to brew extremely clean, distinct, and brightly acidic coffee, which is currently the mainstream choice for pursuing the ultimate hand-brewed flavor.
- **Conical Burrs**: A conical burr rotates within an annular outer burr, using gravity to crush the beans downward. Cone cutters often produce a "bimodal distribution" (a specific group of fines peaks). Although there are more fine powders, these fine powders can bring higher concentration and body, and have a higher fault tolerance rate. They are very suitable for traditional Italian espresso or medium-deep roasted hand brews that pursue a thick taste.

## How to improve your grind?

If you are unable to upgrade your grinder for the time being, the following methods can help you reduce the damage caused by uneven grinding:

1. **Sifting**: Use a special coffee powder sieve (such as Kruve) to sift out fine powder (< 400 microns) before brewing. Although some coffee powder will be wasted, it can immediately significantly improve the cleanliness of the flavor.
2. **Lower the water temperature**: Because fine powder is easily over-extracted, lowering the water temperature by 2-3°C can slow down the release of bitter substances from the fine powder.
3. **Less Agitation**: Injecting water too vigorously or stirring with a spoon will accelerate the deposition of fine powder downwards and clog the filter paper. Try using a gentle, low water column.

Uniform grinding is the physical cornerstone of the ultimate coffee. When you have extremely consistent ground coffee, all the extraction variables (water temperature, time, ratio) make sense.
