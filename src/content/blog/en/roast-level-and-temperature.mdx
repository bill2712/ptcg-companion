Among the variables of hand-brewed coffee, "water temperature" is often the most confusing one for novices. Some people on the Internet say that 93°C is the perfect temperature, while others say that 88°C is not bitter. Who is right?

The truth is, there is no absolutely perfect temperature. **The choice of water temperature depends entirely on the "Roast Level" of the coffee beans**. The degree of roasting determines the physical structure and chemical composition of the beans, and these two directly determine how much energy (temperature) we need to extract the essence. This article will take you deep into the cellular level and analyze the inseparable physical relationship between roasting degree and water temperature.

## Changes in the microcosm during the baking process

When green beans enter a high-temperature roaster, they will undergo a series of violent physical and chemical changes. Among them, the ones that have the greatest impact on brewing are the "Maillard Reaction", "Caramelization" and "Thermal Degradation of Cell Walls".

1. **Water Evaporation and Volume Expansion**: The water in the green beans is heated into water vapor, generating huge internal pressure and eventually bursting the cell wall (this is the "First Crack" we often hear).
2. **Carbonization and Degradation of Cellulose**: As the roasting time of raw beans, which are originally as hard as rocks, increases, the lignocellulose will gradually be destroyed and become brittle, and many tiny holes will be formed inside (the porous structure increases).
3. **Conversion of flavor substances**: from light roasted organic acids (malic acid, citric acid), to medium roasted caramel and nutty aromas, to dark roasted carbonized bitter substances.

By understanding these changes, we can fully grasp the logic of temperature setting.

---

## Light Roast: A wall breaker that needs high heat

**Recommended water temperature: 92°C - 96°C**

Light roasted coffee is usually brewed at or just after the first crack.
- **Physical state**: The cell wall has a very low degree of degradation, the texture is very hard, and there are very few pores inside.
- **Chemical state**: Retains the most organic acids and floral and fruity aroma substances, but these substances are firmly locked deep in the strong cell wall.

**Why is high temperature needed? **
Faced with such a solid physical structure, low-temperature water is unable to penetrate the cell wall and can only wash out a small amount of acidic substances on the surface, causing the entire cup of coffee to taste extremely sour, watery, and lacking in sweetness (typical under-extraction).
We need radical high temperatures of up to 92°C or even 96°C to use powerful thermal energy to "collision" and soften the cell walls, forcibly pulling out the sweet substances and body in the depths to balance the bright acid value. Don't worry about the bitter taste, because light roasted beans themselves rarely produce carbonized bitter substances.

---

## Medium Roast: Finding the perfect balance

**Recommended water temperature: 88°C - 92°C**

Medium roast coffee continues to develop after the first crack and may be approaching or just entering the second crack.
- **Physical state**: The cell wall has been degraded to a certain extent, the texture is lighter and crispier, and the pores have increased.
- **Chemical state**: Organic acids begin to decrease, and the caramelization reaction reaches its peak, producing a large amount of sweet mid-section substances such as stone fruits, chocolate, and caramel, and also begins to produce trace amounts of bitter compounds.

**Why is medium temperature needed? **
At this point the beans have become relatively easy to extract. If you continue to use a high temperature of 96°C, the water will drive in too easily, bringing out the newly formed bitter substances at the end, covering up the charming sweetness of caramel.
Lowering the temperature to 88°C-92°C can gently dissolve a large amount of sugar and caramel aroma, while avoiding excessive stimulation of the bitter taste at the end, creating the most rounded, sweet and balanced taste.

---

## Dark Roast: Low-temperature extraction to avoid disaster

**Recommended water temperature: 80°C - 85°C**

Dark roasted coffee has entered the second explosion intensive period, and coffee oil will even seep from the surface.
- **Physical state**: The cell wall has been completely destroyed, the interior is full of huge holes like a sponge, and the texture is extremely crispy.
- **Chemical State**: There is almost no acidity, and the caramelized substances also begin to be carbonized in large quantities, transforming into strong burnt bitterness, smoky taste and heavy body.

**Why is low temperature needed? **
Dark roasted beans are extremely easy to extract. As soon as the water comes into contact with these "sponges", the substances will pour out instantly. If you use hot water over 90°C to brew a dark roast, it will be a disaster - the high temperature will instantly detonate all the carbonized bitterness and astringency, making the entire cup of coffee become as hard to drink as coke.
We have to use a low temperature of 80°C to 85°C (there are even schools that use 75°C). Low temperature can greatly reduce the dissolution efficiency. It is like a gentle filter, bringing out only the remaining caramel sweetness and rich chocolate body in the dark roasted beans, while leaving the unpleasant burnt bitterness in the powder residue.

## Advanced gameplay of Temperature Profiling

After understanding the principle of temperature, more advanced players will try "variable temperature brewing".
Because coffee is extracted first sour, then sweet, then bitter. We can find you at:
1. **Steaming and first half of water injection**: Using a high temperature of 93°C, the most charming floral and fruity aroma and bright acidity are strongly extracted.
2. **Water filling in the second half**: Add a little room temperature water to the hand brewing kettle and lower the water temperature to 85°C. Low-temperature water can effectively inhibit the release of bitter substances at the end of extraction, ensuring the cleanliness of the entire cup of coffee.

## in conclusion

Water temperature is not a rigid number, but the throttle in your hand. **The harder the beans and the lighter the color, the deeper the accelerator (high temperature); the crispier the beans and the darker the color, the lighter the accelerator (low temperature)**.
