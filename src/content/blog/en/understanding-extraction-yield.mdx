The brewing of specialty coffee is essentially a sophisticated chemical extraction experiment. The moment hot water comes into contact with coffee powder, a complex physical and chemical reaction of "dissolution" begins quietly. In this reaction, there is a decisive quantitative indicator, which we call “Extraction Yield (EY)”. Mastering the extraction rate is the key to ultimate flavor.

## What is Extraction Yield?

Simply put, extraction rate refers to "what proportion of coffee solids are dissolved by water and end up in your coffee cup."

About **30% of a roasted coffee bean is water-soluble** (this includes caffeine, chlorogenic acid, various sugars, lipids and aromatic compounds), while the remaining **70% is insoluble wood fiber**.
If we completely dissolve these 30% of soluble substances during the brewing process, the cup of coffee will be filled with extremely strong bitter, astringent and woody flavors, making it difficult to drink.

Therefore, after countless blind tests and scientific analyses, coffee masters have found the widely recognized "golden extraction range" - that is, **18% to 22%**.

### Phased changes in extraction

When water dissolves coffee powder, it does not extract all the flavors equally, but in a clear order:

1. **The first stage (front stage/fruit acid and aroma)**: The most easily soluble substances are the substances with the smallest molecular weight and the strongest polarity. This contains organic acids (such as citric acid, malic acid) and light floral and fruity aromas. This is why the front end of a espresso (Ristretto) is always acidic and bright.
2. **The second stage (middle/sweetness and body)**: Next are sugar compounds, caramelization substances and most of the flavor main bodies that are dissolved, which bring sweetness and thick body to the coffee.
3. **The third stage (tail section/bitterness and astringency)**: The last extracted substances are usually larger molecules and more difficult to dissolve, including tannins that cause bitterness, carbonized flavors caused by heavy roasting, and wood fiber flavors.

## Sensory characteristics of over- and under-extraction

To accurately control extraction, you must first be able to "diagnose" the current extraction status by relying on your sense of taste. This is usually divided into two extremes:

### Under-extraction — Less than 18%
Underextraction occurs when the hot water doesn't have enough time or energy to dissolve the sweetness in the midsection.
**Flavor Characteristics**:
- **Sour and pungent**: Unpleasant strong sour taste, lack of sweetness.
- **Saltyness**: Sometimes there is a strange salty taste similar to sea salt or soy sauce, which is especially common in light roasted coffee.
- **Thin and boring**: Finish is short, watery and lacks body.

### Over-extraction — above 22%
When the water is in contact with the coffee powder for too long, the water temperature is too high, or the grinding is too fine and the resistance is too great, the water will bring out the bitter substances in the tail section.
**Flavor Characteristics**:
- **Dry throat biting**: After drinking, there will be astringency on the tongue or throat, like drinking underripe persimmons or astringent tea.
- **Heavy Bitterness**: Covers up all the delicate floral and fruity aromas, leaving only a lingering bitterness.
- **Cloudy and Hollow**: Flavors are mixed together, losing layering and clarity.

##Ideal Extraction: 18% - 22% The Art of Balance

When the extraction rate falls in the golden range, the acid value and sweetness reach a perfect balance.
The coffee at this time will appear:
- **Clarity**: You can easily identify the flavor notes marked on the packaging, such as jasmine, peach or earl gray tea.
- **Sweet, lingering finish**: After drinking, saliva will continue to secrete in the mouth, with a pleasant caramel or fruity sweetness.
- **Round mouthfeel**: The liquid feels smooth and quality on the tongue.

## How to use the four major variables to adjust the extraction rate?

Don’t be discouraged if you brew a cup of coffee with a flawed flavor. That’s the beauty of pour-over coffee—you can fix it by adjusting the variables. The following are the four most core extraction variables:

### 1. Grind Size — Control surface area
Grinding is the most direct factor affecting extraction.
- **Fineer Grinding (Finer)**: Increases the total contact surface area between coffee powder and water, speeds up the dissolution rate, and **increases the extraction rate**. If your coffee is too acidic and thin, adjust the grind size to a finer size.
- **Coarser grinding (Coarser)**: reduces the surface area, slows down the extraction, and allows the water flow to pass through faster, **lower the extraction rate**. If your coffee tastes dry and bitter, adjust the grind to a coarser size.

### 2. Water Temperature — Extraction Engine
The temperature of the hot water provides the kinetic energy needed to dissolve the substance.
- **Increase water temperature (Hotter)**: The higher the water temperature, the stronger the extraction ability and the faster the extraction speed. Light roast coffee, in particular, often requires high temperatures of 92°C-96°C to help dissolve the tough cell walls.
- **Lower water temperature (Cooler)**: The lower the water temperature, the worse the extraction efficiency, especially the difficulty in bringing out the bitter substances in the tail. The cell walls of dark roasted coffee are extremely puffy, so it is recommended to lower the temperature to 85°C-88°C for brewing to avoid over-extraction.

### 3. Time (Brew Time) — Extraction progress bar
The longer the contact time between water and powder, the more substances will dissolve.
In hand brewing, time is usually controlled indirectly by "grinding degree" and "water injection method". If you deliberately slow down the water filling speed or increase the number of water breaks (such as the four-six method), the total brewing time will be extended, thereby **increasing the extraction rate**. On the contrary, continuous large water column rapid flushing will **reduce the extraction rate**.

### 4. Brew Ratio – the lever of concentration and extraction
This is a commonly misunderstood concept. "Concentration (TDS)" and "Extraction Yield (EY)" are different.
- If you brew with a **1:15** ratio.
- If you switch to **1:18** (same powder, more water): More clean water will absorb more coffee substances like a sponge, which will **increase the extraction rate** (reduce under-extraction), but because the amount of water increases, the overall **concentration will decrease**, and the drink will taste lighter but the extraction will be more complete.
- Finding a suitable ratio of powder to water is to strike a balance between "thickness of taste" and "completeness of flavor extraction".
