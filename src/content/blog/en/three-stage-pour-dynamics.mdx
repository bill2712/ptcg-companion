In front of the bar counter of a specialty coffee shop, you must have seen the barista holding a hand brewing pot and pouring hot water into the filter cup in an elegant manner, then pausing, and then filling the water again. This method of injecting hot water in batches is called "Pulsing".

Among the many water-cutting methods, the "three-stage water filling method" is the most classic, widely used, and extremely fault-tolerant standard brewing structure.
But why don’t we just pour the water out all at once? Behind the segmented water injection, there are actually very rigorous chemical reactions and fluid mechanics principles hidden. This article will completely decipher the scientific secrets of three-stage water injection for you.

## Why can't it be done in one stroke (one injection of water)?

Before discussing the three stages, let us first understand what problems will be encountered in "continuous water injection".
When ground coffee comes into contact with hot water, it releases large amounts of carbon dioxide (especially freshly roasted beans). If we inject a large amount of hot water at once, the gas will rush out and form huge bubbles. These bubbles will push the coffee powder upward, causing the water flow to be unable to evenly contact the lower coffee powder, resulting in serious **uneven extraction**.

In addition, the filter bowl has a limited capacity. Injecting all the water at once will cause the water level to rise to an extremely high level. Excessive water pressure will force the water to quickly slip through the gaps at the edge of the filter paper (the path of least resistance). This is called the "bypass effect", which will cause the brewed coffee to have an extremely thin and watery feel.

Therefore, water injection is broken down into three clear stages in order to accurately control "exhaust gas", "extraction concentration" and "flavor extraction rate".

---

## The first stage: Bloom—exhaust and channel pre-construction

**Phase Goal**: Exhaust carbon dioxide, moisten the powder bed, and establish a uniform capillary channel for subsequent extraction.
**Water amount recommendation**: 2 to 3 times the weight of the coffee powder (for example, 15g of powder, pour 30g - 45g of water).
**Time**: 30 seconds to 45 seconds.

### The Chemistry and Physics of Steaming
During the roasting process, up to 2% by weight of carbon dioxide gas is produced inside the coffee beans. These gases are locked into the bean's cellular structure.
When hot water first comes into contact with room temperature coffee grounds, the heat energy causes the gases to expand rapidly and be expelled. You will see the powder bed swell like breathing. This is called "Bloom".

**Carbon dioxide is the enemy of water**. The air bubbles repel water, preventing hot water from penetrating into the coffee grounds’ cell walls. If there is no proper steaming, the subsequent main water injection will not be able to effectively dissolve the flavor substances.

More importantly, during steaming, a small amount of hot water will shuttle between the powder particles through capillary action, allowing all powder particles to absorb water and expand evenly. It's like sprinkling a little water on dry soil to keep it moist so that it doesn't run right off the surface when heavy rain comes. Uniform wetting can effectively prevent subsequent fatal "channeling".

## Second stage: Main extraction (Main Pour) — stirring and high-concentration extraction

**Phase Goal**: Use the kinetic energy of the water column to tumble the powder bed and extract the core acidity, sweetness and aromatic substances.
**Water volume recommendation**: Inject to about 60% of the total water volume.
**Time**: Approximately 1 minute to 1 minute 15 seconds to complete.

### Fluid mechanics of agitation
When the steaming is over and the exhaust is complete, the coffee powder is in the state most eager to be dissolved.
In the second stage, we usually use a larger and heavier water column (increasing the initial velocity of the water flow) to perform water injection in a circle. When the water column hits the powder bed, it will produce "turbulence", which is called **agitation** in fluid mechanics.

The function of stirring is to break the "high-concentration liquid boundary layer" surrounding each coffee powder.
Imagine you are making tea. If the tea bag remains still, the concentration of tea around the tea bag will become very high, causing the flavor inside to be unable to continue to dissolve. If you stir with a spoon, fresh water will replace the highly concentrated tea water and speed up the dissolution.

In the main extraction stage, strong turbulence allows fresh hot water to continuously wash the surface of the coffee powder, maximizing the extraction of the floral and fruity aroma, organic acid and caramel sweetness in the middle and front stages. This is the main source of the "strength" and "flavor core" of the entire cup of coffee.

## The third stage: Final Pour - dilution and extraction control

**Phase Goal**: Maintain water levels, gently extract remaining flavors, and dilute the entire cup of coffee to the proper consistency.
**Water Volume Recommendations**: Add remaining 40% of water volume to target total weight.
**Time**: Fill in water slowly and finish dripping in about 2 minutes to 2 minutes and 30 seconds.

### Gentle penetrating extraction
After the first two stages, 70%-80% of the high-quality flavor in the coffee powder has been extracted. Most of the remaining substances are components such as wood fibers and tannins that have larger molecules and are more difficult to dissolve, which can bring bitterness.

Therefore, the hydrodynamic strategy for the third stage must change: **minimize spoilers**.
We use a very soft water column instead, or lower the water injection height and slowly inject water in a circle or even at a fixed point in the center. We no longer want to stir up the bottom layer of coffee grounds, but simply allow the water to maintain a stable water level pressure above the ground bed.

At this time, the water mainly "percolates" through the powder bed through gravity. It will gently bring out a little substance that supports the body. More importantly, this part of the water will act as a **diluent** to dilute the ultra-high concentration coffee liquid extracted in the second stage to the most comfortable and balanced TDS (about 1.2% - 1.45%) for the human taste.

If the water column is too large and the stirring is too vigorous in the final stage, all the bitter substances at the bottom will be ruthlessly washed out, ruining the cleanliness of the entire cup of coffee.

## Conclusion: The Art of Rhythm and Control

The three-stage water injection method perfectly complies with the chemical laws of coffee extraction:
1. **Steaming**: Remove obstacles (exhaust) and prepare the passage.
2. **Main Extraction**: A strong attack to capture the most beautiful sweet and sour essence.
3. **Tail**: Finish gently to dilute the concentration and avoid bitterness.

Master the fluid mechanics behind this and you won't just be blindly drawing circles when brewing. You can freely adjust the water ratio and water column size of these three stages according to the roasting degree and freshness of the beans.
